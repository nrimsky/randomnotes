Time-dependence of stochastic processes

Free diffusion

The Fokker-Planck equation describing the diffusion of a particle with a diffusion coefficient \( B(x) = 2D \), in an environment at temperature \( T \) and subject to a drift term \( A(x) = \frac{D}{k_BT}f(x) \), where \( f(x) \) is the applied force, is given by: $$ \frac{\partial p(x,t) }{ \partial t } = -\frac{D}{k_BT}\frac{\partial }{ \partial x} (f(x)p(x,t)) + D \frac{\partial^2 }{ \partial x^2 } p(x,t)$$ Over time, diffusive motion makes the particle’s position increasingly difficult to predict given the knowledge of where it was initially. It is this increasing uncertainty that is described by the broadening of the distribution with time. This spreading out is actually a relaxation towards equilibrium; with no potential influencing the particle, it should be equally likely to be anywhere. Thus equilibrium predicts a flat distribution.

The Langevin equation

The idea of the Langevin equation is to take a complex stochastic process and split its updates into a potentially complex deterministic part, and a simple stochastic part. A single update for a trajectory \( x(t) \) sampled from \( X(t) \) is: $$ \Delta x = A(x)\Delta t + \sqrt{2D\Delta t} \Delta w$$ where \( \Delta w \) is a sample from \( \Delta W \), a Gaussian random variable with mean 0 and variance 1. We can obtain sample trajectories from a continuous-time, continuous state process numerically by repeatedly applying the Langevin equation as an update rule. For a sufficiently small \( \Delta t \), the generated trajectories \( x(t) \) will approximate a representative sample of the true trajectories.

Dynamics of population processes

The simple representation of the Master equation for a population process as $$ \frac{dp_x(t)}{dt} = -(\lambda(x) + \mu(x))p_x(t) + \lambda(x-1)p_{x-1}(t) + \mu(x+1)p_{x+1}(t) $$ allows us to multiply both sides of the equation by \( x \), and sum over all values of \( x \), to derive the dynamics of \( \langle X \rangle \), the expected value of \( X \). Multiplying by x and summing over all possible values, we find that all of the sums on the right-hand side are expectations of quantities like \( x \) or \( x^2 \). This means \( \frac{d\langle X(t) \rangle}{dt} \) can be found using the ODE: $$ \frac{d\langle X(t) \rangle}{dt} = \lambda_0 - \mu_0\langle X(t) \rangle $$ We see that, even though individual trajectories of the immigration-death model can ary wildly, the average population obeys an extremely simple ODE, reaching a stable value at \( \langle X(t) \rangle = \lambda_0/\mu_0 \).

Poisson point processes

A Poisson point process is a population process that can only increase.
In a general point process, the length of time between event \( n \) and event \( n+1 \) could depend in an arbitrarily complicated way on the history of the process. However, the simplest possible model is one in which the total number of events observed, \( N(t) \) is a continuous-time, discrete-state Markov process with a constant rate of transition \(\nu \) from \( N = n \) to \( N = n + 1 \). This type of model is known as a Poisson point process, and it is essentially an extremely simple population process in which the population can only increase by 1, and does so with a constant propensity.

\( M \) is a random variable describing the number of events in a time window \( \Delta t \) of a Poisson point process. The distribution of \( M \) is a function of \(\nu \) and \( \Delta t \): \( p_m(\nu, \Delta t) \). This distribution is a Poisson distribution with mean \( \bar{m} = \nu\Delta t \): $$ p^{\text{po}}_m(\nu\Delta t) = \frac{ \exp(-\nu\Delta t)(\nu\Delta t)^m}{m!} $$

The time gap between events in a Poisson process is an exponentially distributed continuous random variable (as in any continuous time Markov process). In an exponential distribution, the variance is equal to the mean squared. The number of events in a given time window follows a (discrete) Poisson distribution \( p^{\text{po}}_m(\nu\Delta t) \).
