Continuous-state Markov processes

Many systems have naturally continuous degrees of freedom, for example a diffusing particle. In this case, the probability of each state can no longer be represented by a finite-dimensional vector. Instead, we consider integrals of continuous function rather than sums of vector components. Thus the Master equation takes the form: $$ \frac{\partial p(x,t)}{\partial t} = \int_{A_X} dx'k(x|x')p(x',t) - k(x'|x)p(x,t) $$
  • \( k(x'|x) \) is the transition probability per unit time per unit \( x \)
  • \( A_X \) is the domain of all possible values of \( X \)
  • \( p(x,t) \) is a probability density, therefore \( \int_{A_X}p(x,t)dx = 1 \)

Fokker-Planck equation

When only short-range jumps in state are possible, as for a diffusing particle, it makes sense to Taylor expand \( k(x'|x) \), while allows use to rearrange the Master equation to the Fokker-Planck equation: $$ \frac{\partial p(x,t)}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(A(x)p(x,t)) + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}(B(x)p(x,t)) $$ where \( A(x) \) and \( B(x) \) are coefficients related to the details of \( k(x'|x) \).
  • \( A(x) \) is zero if transitions are intrinsically equally likely to be forwards or backwards ( \( k(x'|x) \) is symmetric about \( x \) ).
  • When \( A(x) \neq 0 \) the system tends to move asymmetrically in one direction or the other. Therefore, the first term of the Fokker-Planck equation is known as the "drift term".
  • If we let \( B(x) = 2D \) be a constant and \( A(x) = 0 \) then we see that: $$ \frac{\partial p(x,t)}{\partial t} = D\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}p(x,t) $$ This is known as the Diffusion equation and second term of the Fokker-Planck equation is known as the "diffusion term".

The stationary distribution for continuous-state, continuous-time processes

We can find the stationary distribution of a continuous-state, continuous-time Markov process by solving the ODE $$ -\frac{d}{dx}(A(x)\pi(x)) + D\frac{d^2}{dx^2}(\pi(x)) = 0 $$ This ODE can also be used to verify whether a candidate probability density \( \pi(x) \) is a stationary distribution, by checking whether 0 is obtained when it is substituted in. Normalisation and relevant boundary conditions must also be checked.
